method  ·  multi-objective backbones

Four MOO engines, one m-axis.

The framework ships four MOEAs so the same surrogate / acquisition stack can sit on different multi-objective backbones — each backbone has a documented sweet spot in the number-of-objectives axis. No single algorithm dominates from m=2 to m=15; SAEA needs the choice.

figure 3.4.a

Applicability across m.

Bars span each algorithm's documented operating range; the heavier outline marks the sweet spot — where its specific mechanism (crowding, reference points, decomposition, APD) gives the cleanest signal.

01  ·  bi/tri-objective baseline

NSGA-II

Deb, Pratap, Agarwal & Meyarivan 2002 — IEEE TEVC

sweet m 2 — 3

Non-dominated sorting layers + crowding distance for diversity. Workhorse of architectural optimisation literature; crowding loses resolution past m=4 because nearly all points become near-isolated in high-d objective space.

mating binary tournament  ·  SBX + polynomial mutation
env sel. rank, then crowding distance within rank

Deb 2002 — IEEE TEVC 6(2)

02  ·  many-objective reference

NSGA-III

Deb & Jain 2014 — IEEE TEVC

sweet m 3 — 10

Same non-dominated sort, but crowding replaced by reference points (Das–Dennis simplex grid). Each individual associates to its nearest reference; ref-point niching scales where crowding fails.

mating binary tournament  ·  SBX + polynomial mutation
env sel. rank + ref-point niching  ·  p divisions

Deb & Jain 2014[pdf] — IEEE TEVC 18(4)

03  ·  decomposition + DE

MOEA/D-DE

Zhang & Li 2007 + Li & Zhang 2009 (DE variant)

sweet m 2 — 8

No non-dominated sort. Decomposes MO into N scalar sub-problems via Tchebycheff / PBI; neighbours collaborate. DE crossover replaces SBX — stronger exploration on irregular / disconnected / concave Pareto fronts.

mating neighbour selection  ·  polynomial mutation
env sel. aggregation function (Tchebycheff / PBI)  ·  nr replacement

Li & Zhang 2009

04  ·  many-objective angular

RVEA

Cheng, Jin, Olhofer & Sendhoff 2016

sweet m 5 — 15

Angle-Penalised Distance instead of dominance: APD = ‖f‖·(1+θ·γα) penalises convergence and angular deviation simultaneously. Reference vectors adapt periodically to track ideal/nadir drift — robust on disparately-scaled objectives.

mating random selection  ·  SBX + polynomial mutation
env sel. APD niche-by-niche  ·  ref vectors adapt every fr·Gmax

Cheng 2016 — IEEE TEVC 20(5)

claim
Four MOEAs cover m = 2 through 15 with no single algorithm dominating — dominance, decomposition and angle-penalised distance each handle a different region of the m axis. The same surrogate and acquisition stack swap onto any of them; the backbone is a parameter, not a commitment.
