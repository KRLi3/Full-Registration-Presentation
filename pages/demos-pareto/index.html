DEMONSTRATIONS CHAPTER 2  ·  PRIMER  ·  PARETO IN MOTION

primer  ·  building intuition before formalism

Four moving pictures, no equations.

For viewers new to multi-objective optimisation: each panel is a small loop showing one core idea — what candidates look like, what dominance means, what one generation does, and what online infill picks. Hover any panel to focus; the loops are short and self-explanatory.

01  ·  the population

What we start with.

A population of candidate designs, scattered across the two-objective plane. Each dot is one design — both objectives are minimised, so closer to the lower-left is better. At this point we don't know which are good.

02  ·  what survives

The Pareto front.

A point is dominated if some other point is at least as good in every objective and strictly better in one. The non-dominated points form the Pareto front — the burgundy chain. Greyed dots are dominated; they get culled.

03  ·  one generation

How a child is born.

One iteration, four steps. Pick two parents from the population → combine (crossover) gives a child somewhere between them → perturb (mutation) jitters the child slightly. Do this N times and you have the next generation.

04  ·  survivor selection

Who makes it to the next generation.

Pool of 20 → keep 10. Sort by non-dominated rank and add whole fronts until one would overflow. That last (critical) front is filtered by crowding distance — keep the most spread-out (filled ochre); cull the rest (ringed ochre) and all higher ranks (grey ✗).

claim
Multi-objective optimisation is not a scalar contest — it is a frontier of mutually-incomparable trade‑offs. Dominance, ranking, and infill all have a shape before they have a formula.
